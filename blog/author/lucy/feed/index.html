For years, weve been hearing the same thing: cash is on the way out.

Contactless payments, mobile wallets and digital banking have transformed how we spend money day to day. But when it comes to travel, the reality is very different.

A recent industry report has revealed that 69% of travellers still use cash when theyre abroad, highlighting just how important physical currency remains when you leave home.

For anyone who travels regularly, this wont come as a surprise.

Because while cards are convenient, cash still plays a key role in making travel smoother, easier and often less stressful.

Do People Still Use Cash Abroad?

In short, yes and in many cases, more than you might expect.

The report shows that travellers arent choosing between cash or card. Instead, most people use both.

This mix and match approach reflects how people actually spend when theyre away:

  • Cards for hotels, restaurants and larger purchases

  • Cash for everyday spending, tips and smaller transactions

Its practical, flexible and gives you a backup if anything goes wrong.

Why Cash Still Matters When You Travel

Even in destinations where card payments are widely accepted, cash continues to play an important role.

1. Not Everywhere Accepts Cards

Local markets, taxis and smaller cafés often prefer cash, particularly outside major tourist areas.

2. It Helps You Stay on Budget

Using cash can make it easier to keep track of what youre spending day to day.

3. Its a Reliable Backup

Cards can be declined, blocked or lost. Having local currency gives you peace of mind.

4. You Can Avoid Unexpected Fees

Using your card abroad can come with charges or less favourable exchange rates, depending on your provider.

Travellers Are Planning Their Travel Money Earlier

Another key trend from the report is that travellers are becoming more organised.

More people are now sorting their travel money before they leave, rather than leaving it until the airport.

And its easy to see why.

Planning ahead means you can:

  • Avoid last-minute stress

  • Have cash ready as soon as you arrive

  • Take advantage of more competitive exchange rates

Leaving it until the airport is often the most expensive option, and its something more travellers are now choosing to avoid.

Cash vs Card Abroad: Its About Balance

The idea that travel is becoming completely cashless doesnt reflect real-life experience.

Most travellers take a balanced approach:

  • Use cards for convenience

  • Carry cash for flexibility

Having both means youre prepared for any situation, whether youre paying for dinner in a city or buying something from a local market.

What This Means for Your Next Trip

The takeaway is simple: cash is still a key part of travelling abroad.

While digital payments are growing, physical currency continues to play an important role in how people spend overseas.

For years, weve been told the same story: cash is disappearing.

Contactless cards, mobile wallets and digital payments have transformed how we spend money. In some cities, you can go days without touching a banknote.

But Switzerland has just reminded the world that physical cash still matters.

In a recent vote reported by Politico, Swiss voters backed a proposal to protect access to cash by embedding it in the countrys constitution. The decision sends a clear signal that, even in one of the worlds most advanced financial systems, people still value the option of paying with notes and coins.

And for travellers, thats actually very good news.

Why Switzerland Is Protecting Cash

The proposal came after a campaign arguing that cash should remain a guaranteed part of everyday life.

Supporters believe physical money plays an important role in areas such as privacy, financial freedom and resilience if digital payment systems fail.

Rather than adopting the original campaign proposal, Swiss voters supported a government counter-proposal that still achieves the same core aim: ensuring cash remains available and usable in the future.

In practical terms, it means the Swiss government must continue to make sure that banknotes and coins remain in circulation and accessible.

In other words, cash isnt disappearing from Switzerland any time soon.

A Wider European Debate

The Swiss vote reflects a broader conversation taking place across Europe.

Digital payments have grown rapidly in recent years, particularly since the pandemic accelerated the use of contactless cards and mobile apps.

At the same time, many consumers and policymakers are asking whether societies should move too quickly towards a cashless future.

Several European countries are now exploring ways to protect access to cash, particularly for older people, rural communities and those who prefer not to rely entirely on digital banking.

Switzerlands decision shows just how strong public support for cash can still be.

Why Cash Still Matters When You Travel

If you travel regularly, youll already know that relying entirely on cards isnt always the best strategy.

While card payments are widely accepted in many destinations, cash still plays an important role in everyday travel.

For example:

  • Small businesses and markets
    Independent cafés, food stalls and local shops often prefer cash payments.
  • Transport and tipping
    Taxis, guides and small service providers may expect cash, particularly outside major cities.
  • Rural or remote locations
    Card machines and internet connections are not always reliable in remote areas.
  • A simple backup
    Cards can be blocked, lost or declined. Having local currency provides peace of mind.

This is why experienced travellers rarely rely on a single payment method. A combination of cash and cards usually works best.

The Future of Cash Isnt So Simple

Predictions about the death of cash have been around for decades, yet physical money continues to play a role in economies worldwide.

What Switzerlands vote highlights is something many travellers already understand: convenience matters, but choice matters too.

Digital payments are fast and easy. But cash offers flexibility, privacy and reliability when technology isnt available.

That balance is likely to shape the future of payments for years to come.

Having some local currency on hand can make the start of your trip far smoother, especially for transport, tips or small purchases.

Planning a getaway is one of life’s great pleasures. You’ve snagged the flights, drooled over your hotel’s infinity pool on Instagram, and maybe even started a countdown on your phone. But then it hits youthat nagging question lurking in the back of every traveller’s mind: How much cash do I actually need to bring?

Because nobody wants to be that person fumbling for coins at a street food stall, or worse, paying £5 to withdraw 20 from a dodgy airport ATM. Get it wrong and you’re either eating instant noodles for three days or hauling around a wallet so thick it throws your back out.

So let’s figure out how much holiday money you actually need, without the boring spreadsheets or financial jargon.

 

Why the “Card Only” Approach Can Be Costly

In an increasingly digital world, it is tempting to assume a debit card is all you need. While cards are convenient for large purchases like hotel bills, relying on them for everything can be a tactical error for the savvy UK traveller.

  • Hidden Fees: Many standard UK bank accounts charge a foreign transaction fee (often around 3%) every time you tap.
  • The Cash-Only Reality: Whether it is a rustic taverna in Greece, a local market in Marrakesh, or a small tip for a helpful porter, many authentic experiences still require physical currency.
  • Exchange Rate Volatility: Using a card means you get the rate on the day of the transaction. By ordering your currency in advance, you can lock in a great rate when the pound is strong.

 

Planning Your Daily Budget

To work out your total, it helps to break your spending down into four main categories. Every trip is different, but these pillars remain constant.

1. Sustenance: Food and Drink

Consider your accommodation type. If you have breakfast included, your daily spend drops significantly. For a mid-range experience, we generally recommend budgeting for a light lunch, a three-course dinner, and a few drinks.

2. Transport and Getting Around

Will you be using the Metro in Paris, or do you prefer the convenience of local taxis? Don’t forget to budget for the journey from the airport to your accommodation, which is often the most expensive taxi ride of the trip.

3. Sightseeing and Activities

Entry fees for museums, guided tours, or a day trip to a nearby island can add up. It is often wise to set aside a “buffer” for spontaneous excursions you didn’t see coming.

4. The “Treat Yourself” Fund

You are on holiday, after all. Whether it is an extra gelato, a souvenir for the family, or a sunset cocktail, having a dedicated pot for small luxuries ensures you don’t feel guilty about enjoying your time away.

 

Real Budgets for Real Trips

To give you a clearer picture, we have put together some estimated daily spends for three popular types of British holidays. These figures assume you have already paid for your flights and accommodation.

The Weekend City Break

Cities can be surprisingly affordable or famously pricey. In Eastern Europe, your pound will often stretch much further than in Scandinavia or Switzerland. For a three-day trip, many of our customers find that having around £250 to £300 in local currency covers most bases comfortably.

The Week-Long Beach Holiday

Coastal spending tends to be more consistent, but here’s the thing: we can’t tell you precisely what you’ll spend because we don’t know what you’re into.

Are you the type who hunts down 3 tapas and free sunset viewpoints? Or does your ideal afternoon involve a long lunch with wine, followed by a private boat trip? For the seasoned British traveller, the pre-holiday ritual is well-rehearsed: passports checked, suitcases weighed, and the “out of office” firmly switched on. But even for those who fly several times a year, the world of foreign exchange still has a few surprises up its sleeve.

As we move through 2026, the global payment landscape is a bit of a patchwork quilt. While you can pay for a coffee with your watch in London or Stockholm, other parts of the world still operate on a “cash is king” basis. Sometimes, this leads to rather unexpected, and occasionally humorous, situations where your premium credit card is about as useful as a chocolate teapot.

The “Cash Required” Hall of Fame

You might expect to need notes for a three-course dinner, but it is often the smallest transactions that catch travellers off guard. Here are a few instances where physical currency isn’t just a backup; it is the only way forward.

The 50-Cent Toll for Privacy

In many parts of Continental Europe, particularly in Germany and the Netherlands, the “Sanifair” or public toilet systems are a rite of passage. There is nothing quite like the mild panic of reaching the front of a turnstile only to realise it requires a specific coin and doesn’t accept contactless. Keeping a handful of small change is the ultimate “pro-tip” for any European road trip.

Divine Intervention and Wishing Wells

Planning on tossing a coin into the Trevi Fountain or lighting a candle in a Greek Orthodox church? These centuries-old traditions haven’t quite moved into the digital age. If you want to make a wish or show respect in a place of worship, you will need physical coins. A digital tap just doesn’t have the same charm or, usually, a card reader.

The “Sincere” Tip

In destinations like Egypt, Turkey, or even the USA, “baksheesh” or tipping is woven into the fabric of daily life. While large restaurants might add a service charge, the person who helps you with your heavy bags or the guide who shows you a hidden viewpoint cannot “swipe” your gratitude. Small denominations of local currency are essential for ensuring your thanks actually reach the person intended.

When “Cash Only” is the Law of the Land

We often hear that the world is going cashless, but for many popular holiday destinations, the reality is quite different.

In Japan, despite its futuristic reputation, many traditional ryokans (inns) and exquisite local eateries in Kyoto still prefer yen in hand. Similarly, in many Caribbean islands, local markets and beachside shacks often avoid card machines due to spotty island Wi-Fi.

Even in “modern” destinations, you might find:

Market Bargaining: In the souks of Marrakech or the flea markets of Paris, your bargaining power is significantly higher if you have the cash ready to settle the deal there and then.

Island Taxis: From the Greek Isles to the Maldives, local boat transfers and taxis are notorious for “the card machine is broken” scenarios. Having cash avoids a stressful start to your stay.

The “Over-Polished” Card and Other Travel Tales

Weve all heard the stories, or perhaps lived them. The traveller who tries to pay for a 2 bottle of water with a high-tier metal credit card, only to be met with a blank stare from a shopkeeper in a rural village. Or the frustration of a card being declined halfway across the world because the banks fraud algorithm decided your souvenir purchase in Tokyo looked “suspicious.”

There is a certain confidence that comes with carrying a well-organised wallet. Relying purely on tech means you are at the mercy of battery life, signal strength, and international banking systems. Cash, on the other hand, never has a “system outage.”

Being Prepared with the Right Denominations

The secret to stress-free travel isn’t just having cash; it is having the right cash. Arriving in a country with only large, high-value notes can be almost as tricky as having no cash at all. Try paying for a 50p bus fare with a 50 note, and you might find the driver is unable to provide change.

If youve booked a holiday and are keeping half an eye on exchange rates, you may have seen this

recent headline in the Telegraph: Buy your holiday money now. The pound is in for a rocky year.
(You can read the original article here.)

At Travel FX, we speak to people like you every day who are simply trying to answer one question: how do I avoid nasty surprises when Im abroad?

This article isnt financial advice and its not about predicting the markets. Its about helping you think through your options, so you can choose what feels right for your trip.

Why exchange rate headlines matter to you

You dont need to follow the financial pages to feel the impact of currency movements. When the pound weakens, even slightly, it can mean:

  • your spending money doesnt stretch as far

  • everyday holiday costs feel higher than expected

  • youre left wishing youd planned a bit more ahead

We saw this last year when the pound moved around noticeably over short periods. If you had already sorted your travel money, you were largely insulated from those swings. If you waited, you may have ended up paying more for the same amount of currency.

Theres no right or wrong decision, but uncertainty tends to hit hardest when things are left until the last minute.

Buying travel money early is about certainty, not predictions

Its worth being clear. Nobody knows what the pound will do next. Exchange rates move up and down, often without warning.

For many people, buying travel money earlier isnt about trying to beat the market. Its about:

  • knowing what your holiday money will cost

  • avoiding a last-minute rush before you travel

  • taking one more thing off your pre-holiday checklist

Think of it like booking flights. You dont wait until the night before in the hope prices might drop. You book when youre comfortable with the price and move on.

What people like you often choose to do

From our experience, many customers take a simple, practical approach:

  • buying some or all of their travel money once their holiday is booked

  • spreading purchases if they dont want to commit all at once

  • making sure funds are sorted before travelling, rather than relying on airport exchange desks

Its less about perfect timing and more about feeling organised and in control.

Cash, cards or a combination?

Most people now travel with a mix. Cards are convenient for hotels and larger payments, while cash is still useful for taxis, tips and smaller day-to-day spends.

Whatever you prefer, planning ahead gives you more choice and usually better value than leaving everything until you arrive or pass through the airport.

Important note

This article is intended to help you think about practical travel money options. It is not financial advice and it does not make predictions about future exchange rate movements. Any decision about if or when to buy travel money should be based on your own circumstances and comfort level.

A gentle reminder from Travel FX

If exchange rate headlines are making you feel uneasy, it may be worth thinking ahead. Sorting your travel money early can mean one less thing to worry about and more confidence in your holiday budget.

With straightforward online ordering and secure home delivery, Travel FX makes this part simple, so you can focus on enjoying your trip.

Bottom line: you cant control the pound, but you can control how prepared you feel before you travel. For many people, that peace of mind is worth planning for.

As the year wraps up and brands everywhere start sharing their 2025 highlights, we thought wed jump on the bandwagon and look back at the year from a slightly different angle.

Not through airport queues or suitcase sizes, but through the lens we know best: travel money.

2025 was a big year for UK travellers. From long-awaited bucket-list trips to quick city breaks and winter sun escapes, it was clear that the appetite to travel remained strong. And while digital payments continued to grow, one thing stayed reassuringly familiar: cash still had its place.

Heres our take on what 2025 looked like for travellers, currencies, and Travel FX.

Where UK Travellers Went in 2025

Travel patterns in 2025 felt confident and varied. European city breaks remained hugely popular, especially for shorter getaways and shoulder-season travel. Destinations offering great food, culture, and walkable city centres were high on the list.

Long-haul travel also saw a strong return, with many travellers happy to plan further ahead and stay longer once they arrived. Winter sun escapes continued to thrive, as did multi-destination trips where travellers mixed cities, coast, and countryside in one holiday.

In short, UK travellers were out exploring again, and they were doing it thoughtfully.

The Most Bought Currencies of 2025

Certain currencies stood out again this year, reflecting where Brits chose to spend their time.

European currencies remained consistently popular thanks to frequent short trips and easy access. The US Dollar continued to be a firm favourite for long-haul travel, while other destinations outside Europe attracted travellers looking for culture, value, and memorable experiences.

Some of these destinations found themselves in the political or economic headlines during 2025. Interestingly, that did very little to dampen traveller enthusiasm. Most people simply planned ahead, stayed informed, and travelled anyway.

It was a reminder that while news cycles change quickly, the desire to travel tends to be far more resilient.

Exchange Rates, Headlines and Traveller Confidence

If there was one thing that appeared in the news more often in 2025, it was exchange rates.

Despite the headlines, traveller confidence stayed strong. Rather than putting people off, it encouraged smarter planning. Many customers chose to secure their travel money earlier, keep an eye on rates, and avoid last-minute airport exchanges.

It was a year where being prepared paid off.

Cash in a More Digital World

Theres no denying that digital payments are becoming more common. Contactless cards, mobile wallets, and app-based spending all continued to grow in 2025.

But cash did not disappear.

Across many destinations, cash remained essential for markets, tipping, taxis, smaller cafés, rural areas, and day-to-day convenience. For many travellers, having cash alongside cards offered flexibility, reassurance, and better control over spending.

Once again, 2025 proved that cash still earns its place in your travel wallet.

A Big Year for Travel FX

2025 was also an important year for us.

We introduced new payment systems, giving customers more choice and flexibility when managing their travel money and international payments. These additions were made with the same focus weve always had: making things simple, secure, and easy to use.

We were also incredibly proud to be awarded Silver at the British Travel Awards. Being recognised by the people who matter most, our customers, is something we never take for granted. It was a fantastic moment for the whole team and a real highlight of the year.

What 2025 Taught Us About Travel Money

Looking back, a few things stood out:

  • Planning ahead makes travel smoother

  • Trusted providers matter more than ever

  • Cash still plays a vital role abroad

  • Travellers are confident, informed, and adaptable

These lessons shaped how people travelled in 2025 and theyll continue to shape how people prepare for trips in the year ahead.

Looking Ahead

2025 reminded us why we love travel and why travel money still matters.

While the world continues to move towards digital payments, cash remains a reliable companion for UK travellers exploring near or far. As we look ahead to 2026, one thing feels certain: wherever youre going next, being prepared will always make the journey better.

And were proud to be part of that journey.
