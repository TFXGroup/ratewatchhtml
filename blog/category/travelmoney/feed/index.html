I’ll be honest, when my usual Easter escape plans fell through this year, I had a bit of a panic. Dubai’s not happening. The Middle East generally feels complicated right now. And don’t get me started on trying to route through to Asia, the flight connections are either absurdly long or absurdly expensive, sometimes both.

So I started looking west instead. And you know what? The Americas are having a bit of a moment for British travellers. Not the obvious US cities that everyone’s already done, but further south where your pounds still stretch properly and you don’t need two weeks off work just to recover from the jet lag.

Here’s what I’ve learned planning my own trip, plus some hard-won advice about not getting ripped off on exchange rates. Because nothing ruins a holiday faster than realising you paid 15% more than you needed to just for the convenience of grabbing cash at Gatwick.

Costa Rica: The One That Surprised Me

Semana Santa (Easter week) is massive here. The whole country essentially shuts down for religious processions that make our Easter bank holiday look like a quiet Sunday. In towns like Cartago and Heredia, you’ll see these extraordinary parades where locals carry heavy religious statues for hours. It’s not put on for tourists; you’re just allowed to watch.

The currency situation is simple once you understand it. Officially it’s the Costa Rican colón, and that’s what you should use for almost everything. You can pay in US dollars, they accept them pretty much everywhere, but be warned about the variable exchange rates. You could find yourself paying loads more without realising, because everyone uses a different rate and you never know which one you’re getting until it’s too late.

My Suggestion:

Get some colones before you go.

Cards work in proper restaurants, but cash is king everywhere else. Don’t assume you can tap your way through a market in San José.

At Travel FX, we offer competitive rates on Costa Rican colones and 40+ other currencies, with free home delivery on orders over £750.

Mexico: Way More Than the Beach

Everyone’s been to Cancun, or knows someone who has. But Mexico City in spring? Oaxaca for the food? The copper canyon railway? These feel like discoveries again, especially when you can get there without the logistical headaches of flying east.

Mexico’s peso has been weirdly stable against the pound lately, which makes budgeting actually possible.

The cash thing is real though:

Street food, which you absolutely should eat are cash only. We’re talking 30-50 pesos a taco, so you want small notes. Taxis in most cities still prefer cash. Even nice restaurants sometimes claim their card machine is “broken”.

Years ago, I took about 8,000 pesos for a ten-day trip and used local ATMs twice for top-ups. The ATMs charge fees, usually around 100 pesos per withdrawal, so taking out larger amounts makes sense. But walking around with huge wads of cash in Mexico City feels unwise, so it’s a balance.

One tip: airport exchange desks in Mexico are genuinely terrible. Like, insultingly bad. I checked the rate at Cancun airport out of curiosity and actually laughed. Don’t be that person. Colombia: The One I’m Booking Next

Full disclosure: I haven’t been yet. But I’ve spent approximately too many hours researching it, and I’m convinced. 

Cartagena looks stunning. That walled old town, the colours, the heat. The coffee region apparently has these finca stays where you learn about bean production while eating food that’s cheap by UK standards. And it’s still not overrun with British tourists, which counts for a lot in April when half of Europe seems to be on the same flight to Malaga.

The currency situation is eyeopener. The Colombian peso trades at roughly 5,000 to the pound, which means you’re dealing with huge numbers. A decent dinner might cost 80,000 pesos. A taxi ride 15,000. It takes mental adjustment, and that’s easier if you’ve already handled the notes before you arrive.

From what my Colombian friends tell me:

  • Cash is unavoidable outside Bogotá and Medellín’s tourist zones.
  • Everyone wants small bills because nobody has change.
  • Card payments happen, but slowly, with much sighing from staff.

I plan to order my pesos in advance.

What I’ve Learned About Travel Money (The Hard Way)

I’ve made most of the mistakes, so you don’t have to. Airport exchanges are daylight robbery. Not exaggerating. The rates are bad, the fees are worse, and they know you’re trapped. The number of times I’ve seen sensible-looking people calmly accepting terrible deals because “it’s easier”, don’t be one of them.

Your bank card abroad is probably expensive. Most UK banks charge foreign transaction fees, plus ATM fees, plus whatever the local machine charges. It adds up fast. I worked out on one trip that I was paying nearly 6% extra on everything I withdrew. Ordering currency in advance feels old-fashioned in this new digital payment age but in these parts of the world, it works better.

Split your money. But also tell your bank you’re travelling, nothing worse than your card being blocked for “suspicious activity” when you’re trying to pay for a taxi in a foreign country at 11pm.

The Broader Point: Changing Where We Go

There’s something interesting happening with British travel patterns right now. The traditional Easter escape routes (Dubai, Thailand, that whole circuit) feel less straightforward. And rather than just waiting for things to go back to normal, people are exploring alternatives that might actually be better.

Costa Rica has the wildlife and beaches without the Dubai artificiality. Mexico has depth that all-inclusive resorts never reveal. Colombia offers discovery.

The flight times are manageable, roughly 10-11 hours to Costa Rica or Mexico City, often with decent connections through Madrid or the US hubs. The time difference is kinder to your body clock than Bangkok or Singapore. And your money goes further than in the established Middle East destinations.

Yes, you need to think more carefully about currency. Yes, cash matters more than in places where you can use Apple Pay for everything. But that’s part of the experience, engaging with how places actually work rather than floating through sanitised tourist zones.

Practical Stuff: Getting Your Travel Money Sorted

If you’re convinced and starting to plan, here’s my actual process:

  • Check rates a few weeks out. Don’t obsess daily, but get a sense of whether the pound is having a good or bad month against your destination currency.
  • Order about 10 days before you travel. Earlier and you’re gambling on exchange rate movements; later and you pay for express delivery or end up at the airport counter.
  • Keep the receipt. Sometimes you need to show where you got your currency, particularly with larger amounts.
  • Have a backup plan. One card that works, some emergency dollars, knowledge of where the decent ATMs are in your destination.

Final Thoughts

Easter travel doesn’t have to mean fighting through the usual crowds to the usual places. With a bit of flexibility and some advance planning, particularly around your travel money, the Americas offer genuine alternatives that might become your new favourites.

I’m booked for Colombia this April. The pesos order will be purchased 10 days before I fly, the flights are sorted, and I’m genuinely excited about going somewhere that feels like a discovery rather than a default option.

Sometimes the best trips come from having to rethink your plans. This might be one of those times.

For years, weve been told the same story: cash is disappearing.

Contactless cards, mobile wallets and digital payments have transformed how we spend money. In some cities, you can go days without touching a banknote.

But Switzerland has just reminded the world that physical cash still matters.

In a recent vote reported by Politico, Swiss voters backed a proposal to protect access to cash by embedding it in the countrys constitution. The decision sends a clear signal that, even in one of the worlds most advanced financial systems, people still value the option of paying with notes and coins.

And for travellers, thats actually very good news.

Why Switzerland Is Protecting Cash

The proposal came after a campaign arguing that cash should remain a guaranteed part of everyday life.

Supporters believe physical money plays an important role in areas such as privacy, financial freedom and resilience if digital payment systems fail.

Rather than adopting the original campaign proposal, Swiss voters supported a government counter-proposal that still achieves the same core aim: ensuring cash remains available and usable in the future.

In practical terms, it means the Swiss government must continue to make sure that banknotes and coins remain in circulation and accessible.

In other words, cash isnt disappearing from Switzerland any time soon.

A Wider European Debate

The Swiss vote reflects a broader conversation taking place across Europe.

Digital payments have grown rapidly in recent years, particularly since the pandemic accelerated the use of contactless cards and mobile apps.

At the same time, many consumers and policymakers are asking whether societies should move too quickly towards a cashless future.

Several European countries are now exploring ways to protect access to cash, particularly for older people, rural communities and those who prefer not to rely entirely on digital banking.

Switzerlands decision shows just how strong public support for cash can still be.

Why Cash Still Matters When You Travel

If you travel regularly, youll already know that relying entirely on cards isnt always the best strategy.

While card payments are widely accepted in many destinations, cash still plays an important role in everyday travel.

For example:

  • Small businesses and markets
    Independent cafés, food stalls and local shops often prefer cash payments.
  • Transport and tipping
    Taxis, guides and small service providers may expect cash, particularly outside major cities.
  • Rural or remote locations
    Card machines and internet connections are not always reliable in remote areas.
  • A simple backup
    Cards can be blocked, lost or declined. Having local currency provides peace of mind.

This is why experienced travellers rarely rely on a single payment method. A combination of cash and cards usually works best.

The Future of Cash Isnt So Simple

Predictions about the death of cash have been around for decades, yet physical money continues to play a role in economies worldwide.

What Switzerlands vote highlights is something many travellers already understand: convenience matters, but choice matters too.

Digital payments are fast and easy. But cash offers flexibility, privacy and reliability when technology isnt available.

That balance is likely to shape the future of payments for years to come.

Planning a getaway is one of life’s great pleasures. You’ve snagged the flights, drooled over your hotel’s infinity pool on Instagram, and maybe even started a countdown on your phone. But then it hits youthat nagging question lurking in the back of every traveller’s mind: How much cash do I actually need to bring?

Because nobody wants to be that person fumbling for coins at a street food stall, or worse, paying £5 to withdraw 20 from a dodgy airport ATM. Get it wrong and you’re either eating instant noodles for three days or hauling around a wallet so thick it throws your back out.

So let’s figure out how much holiday money you actually need, without the boring spreadsheets or financial jargon.

 

Why the “Card Only” Approach Can Be Costly

In an increasingly digital world, it is tempting to assume a debit card is all you need. While cards are convenient for large purchases like hotel bills, relying on them for everything can be a tactical error for the savvy UK traveller.

  • Hidden Fees: Many standard UK bank accounts charge a foreign transaction fee (often around 3%) every time you tap.
  • The Cash-Only Reality: Whether it is a rustic taverna in Greece, a local market in Marrakesh, or a small tip for a helpful porter, many authentic experiences still require physical currency.
  • Exchange Rate Volatility: Using a card means you get the rate on the day of the transaction. By ordering your currency in advance, you can lock in a great rate when the pound is strong.

 

Planning Your Daily Budget

To work out your total, it helps to break your spending down into four main categories. Every trip is different, but these pillars remain constant.

1. Sustenance: Food and Drink

Consider your accommodation type. If you have breakfast included, your daily spend drops significantly. For a mid-range experience, we generally recommend budgeting for a light lunch, a three-course dinner, and a few drinks.

2. Transport and Getting Around

Will you be using the Metro in Paris, or do you prefer the convenience of local taxis? Don’t forget to budget for the journey from the airport to your accommodation, which is often the most expensive taxi ride of the trip.

3. Sightseeing and Activities

Entry fees for museums, guided tours, or a day trip to a nearby island can add up. It is often wise to set aside a “buffer” for spontaneous excursions you didn’t see coming.

4. The “Treat Yourself” Fund

You are on holiday, after all. Whether it is an extra gelato, a souvenir for the family, or a sunset cocktail, having a dedicated pot for small luxuries ensures you don’t feel guilty about enjoying your time away.

 

Real Budgets for Real Trips

To give you a clearer picture, we have put together some estimated daily spends for three popular types of British holidays. These figures assume you have already paid for your flights and accommodation.

The Weekend City Break

Cities can be surprisingly affordable or famously pricey. In Eastern Europe, your pound will often stretch much further than in Scandinavia or Switzerland. For a three-day trip, many of our customers find that having around £250 to £300 in local currency covers most bases comfortably.

Want a reality check before you pack? Head to Numbeo.com and search your destination. It’ll tell you exactly what locals are paying for a pint, a mid-range dinner, or a taxi across townfar more useful than generic “daily spend” articles written by someone who last visited in 2019.

The Week-Long Beach Holiday

Coastal spending tends to be more consistent, but here’s the thing: we can’t tell you precisely what you’ll spend because we don’t know what you’re into.

Are you the type who hunts down 3 tapas and free sunset viewpoints? Or does your ideal afternoon involve a long lunch with wine, followed by a private boat trip? Self-catering families hitting local markets will spend wildly differently from those ordering poolside cocktails twice a day. Budget for your holiday, not someone else’s.

  • Check the Cost of Living: Before you fly, use Numbeo to benchmark local pricescoffee, meals, transport. It beats guessing.
  • Mix Your Methods: Carry a sensible amount of cash for smaller purchases and use a specialist travel card for larger expenses.
  • Avoid Dynamic Currency Conversion: If a merchant asks if you want to pay in Pounds or the local currency, always choose the local currency to avoid poor “on-the-spot” exchange rates.

 

Final Thoughts

There is no “perfect” amount of money to take, but there is certainly a “smart” way to prepare. By estimating your daily needs and securing your currency in advance, you can step off the plane with confidence, knowing you have the right tools for a fantastic trip.

For the seasoned British traveller, the pre-holiday ritual is well-rehearsed: passports checked, suitcases weighed, and the “out of office” firmly switched on. But even for those who fly several times a year, the world of foreign exchange still has a few surprises up its sleeve.

As we move through 2026, the global payment landscape is a bit of a patchwork quilt. While you can pay for a coffee with your watch in London or Stockholm, other parts of the world still operate on a “cash is king” basis. Sometimes, this leads to rather unexpected, and occasionally humorous, situations where your premium credit card is about as useful as a chocolate teapot.

The “Cash Required” Hall of Fame

You might expect to need notes for a three-course dinner, but it is often the smallest transactions that catch travellers off guard. Here are a few instances where physical currency isn’t just a backup; it is the only way forward.

The 50-Cent Toll for Privacy

In many parts of Continental Europe, particularly in Germany and the Netherlands, the “Sanifair” or public toilet systems are a rite of passage. There is nothing quite like the mild panic of reaching the front of a turnstile only to realise it requires a specific coin and doesn’t accept contactless. Keeping a handful of small change is the ultimate “pro-tip” for any European road trip.

Divine Intervention and Wishing Wells

Planning on tossing a coin into the Trevi Fountain or lighting a candle in a Greek Orthodox church? These centuries-old traditions haven’t quite moved into the digital age. If you want to make a wish or show respect in a place of worship, you will need physical coins. A digital tap just doesn’t have the same charm or, usually, a card reader.

The “Sincere” Tip

In destinations like Egypt, Turkey, or even the USA, “baksheesh” or tipping is woven into the fabric of daily life. While large restaurants might add a service charge, the person who helps you with your heavy bags or the guide who shows you a hidden viewpoint cannot “swipe” your gratitude. Small denominations of local currency are essential for ensuring your thanks actually reach the person intended.

When “Cash Only” is the Law of the Land

We often hear that the world is going cashless, but for many popular holiday destinations, the reality is quite different.

In Japan, despite its futuristic reputation, many traditional ryokans (inns) and exquisite local eateries in Kyoto still prefer yen in hand. Similarly, in many Caribbean islands, local markets and beachside shacks often avoid card machines due to spotty island Wi-Fi.

Even in “modern” destinations, you might find:

Market Bargaining: In the souks of Marrakech or the flea markets of Paris, your bargaining power is significantly higher if you have the cash ready to settle the deal there and then.

Island Taxis: From the Greek Isles to the Maldives, local boat transfers and taxis are notorious for “the card machine is broken” scenarios. Having cash avoids a stressful start to your stay.

The “Over-Polished” Card and Other Travel Tales

Weve all heard the stories, or perhaps lived them. The traveller who tries to pay for a 2 bottle of water with a high-tier metal credit card, only to be met with a blank stare from a shopkeeper in a rural village. Or the frustration of a card being declined halfway across the world because the banks fraud algorithm decided your souvenir purchase in Tokyo looked “suspicious.”

There is a certain confidence that comes with carrying a well-organised wallet. Relying purely on tech means you are at the mercy of battery life, signal strength, and international banking systems. Cash, on the other hand, never has a “system outage.”

Being Prepared with the Right Denominations

The secret to stress-free travel isn’t just having cash; it is having the right cash. Arriving in a country with only large, high-value notes can be almost as tricky as having no cash at all. Try paying for a 50p bus fare with a 50 note, and you might find the driver is unable to provide change.

Bulgaria has been on the path to Euro adoption for years. It joined the European Union in 2007 and entered the Exchange Rate Mechanism (ERM II),  the waiting room for Euro membership,  in 2020.
